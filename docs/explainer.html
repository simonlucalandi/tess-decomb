TESS FFI moving-target photometry · method note

Using ordinary stars to tell a real rotation from an instrument artefact

TESS shakes on a schedule. That shake imprints a fake periodicity on every light curve from the same sector. The de-comb asks a simple question: if this period were real, would the stars know about it too?

The problem

The spacecraft has a heartbeat, and it looks like rotation

Every few days TESS fires its thrusters to bleed off angular momentum from the reaction wheels. Those momentum dumps, plus scattered light from Earth and Moon crossing the field on the orbital cycle, imprint a repeating pattern on the photometry. Because the dumps are near-regular, that pattern does not produce one spurious period, it produces a whole comb of them, at roughly 328.8 / n hours.

An asteroid light curve is short, sparse and noisy. If its strongest periodogram peak lands on a comb tooth, you cannot tell from that light curve alone whether you have found the body's rotation or the spacecraft's housekeeping. This is the trap the whole method exists to defuse, and it is especially dangerous for slow rotators, whose periods live in exactly the range where the teeth are dense.

The idea

The systematics are temporally global

This is the sentence the whole method rests on. The momentum dump does not happen to your asteroid, it happens to the spacecraft. Every source read out on that CCD at that moment gets the same kick. The scattered-light ramp likewise sweeps the whole field.

So the sky is full of witnesses. Ordinary field stars in the same sector are not rotating asteroids, they have no reason to share your candidate period, but they were sitting in the same instrument at the same time. Whatever wiggle they hold in common is, by construction, the instrument talking.

If a periodicity is astrophysical, it belongs to your asteroid alone. If it is instrumental, two hundred stars are already carrying it, and you can measure it from them and subtract it.

The recipe

Four steps, once per sector

  1. Collect witnesses

    Pull about 200 field-star light curves from the same sector. Use SAP flux, not PDCSAP: the standard pipeline already scrubs common-mode trends, and those trends are exactly what we are trying to measure. Quality masking stays deliberately light, because reaction-wheel desaturation cadences are the signal here, not noise to be dropped.

  2. Learn the instrument's vocabulary

    Bin every star onto a shared 30-minute grid and take the SVD of the resulting star × time matrix. The leading right-singular vectors are the shapes the whole field moves in together. Up to eight are cached; the top K = 5 are used. K is a real tuning knob, not a formality, and the section below shows what happens when it is set too high.

  3. Subtract what the stars already explain

    Fit the asteroid's light curve as offset + Σ ck·eigk(t) by linear least squares, interpolating the eigenvectors onto the asteroid's own timestamps, then subtract only the systematics part. The mean level is preserved exactly, so the photometry stays physically meaningful.

  4. Ask the periodogram again

    Re-measure Lomb–Scargle power at the candidate period on the residual. The test is harmonic-aware: it looks at P, P/2 and 2P, anchoring on whichever the sector's genuine detection actually sits at, gated by a Baluev false-alarm probability below 10−3. A real signal barely notices the projection. An instrumental one falls over.

The awkward part

Two pipelines that were never meant to meet

The basis comes from stationary stars in 2-minute SPOC photometry. The target is a moving aperture tracked across full-frame images. Different cadence, different extraction, different code. Borrowing a correction from one and applying it to the other deserves suspicion, and the paragraph says so out loud rather than hiding it.

The defence is physical: the terms being removed are spacecraft- and CCD-wide, not aperture-specific. A thruster firing and a scattered-light ramp are properties of the observatory and the focal plane, so they survive the translation between pipelines. Terms that genuinely are aperture-specific, such as how flux spills across pixel boundaries as the asteroid drifts, are not shared with the stars and are not what this step claims to fix.

That is an argument, not a proof, which is why it is settled empirically by injecting known signals and checking they come back.

The verdict

Two numbers decide, and there is a deliberate gap between them

After projection you have the fractional drop in periodogram power, and the R² of how much of the light curve the systematics model actually explained. A large drop with a model that explained nothing is not evidence, it is noise, which is why both are required to kill.

SURVIVED  drop ≤ 15% KILLED  drop ≥ 50% and R² ≥ 0.3 EVERYTHING ELSE  inconclusive / review

Does it work

Inject known signals and count what you break

The honest failure mode of any systematics removal is eating the science. So the validation injects real and synthetic rotation signals into real light curves, including signals placed directly on comb teeth where the method has the best excuse to destroy them, and counts how often a genuine signal gets killed.

The asymmetry, and it matters

Run the mirror test, feeding it 46 detections that are known comb artefacts, and it kills only 6.5% of them: a false-survive rate near 61%. So the two verdicts carry very different weight. KILLED is strong evidence and can be acted on. SURVIVED is weak evidence, and means only that this particular test failed to convict, not that the period is proven real. Reporting it any other way would overstate the tool.

Where it stops working

Long periods and short sectors

The same logic caps K. At K = 8 the basis becomes flexible enough to start fitting the asteroid's own rotation: in validation, a confirmed 108 h rotation lost 4.7% of its power and a synthetic 90 h injection came back with 83% of its amplitude and a period error 150 times worse than at K = 5. More components remove more instrument, and then they start removing the science.

In short

stars in the same sector share the instrument's systematics → build a basis from ~200 of them (SVD, top K=5) → least-squares subtract it from the asteroid → re-measure LS power at P, P/2, 2P (Baluev FAP < 1e-3) drop ≤ 15% → SURVIVED (astrophysical) drop ≥ 50% and R² ≥ 0.3 → KILLED (instrumental) anything else → REVIEW (not resolved)
